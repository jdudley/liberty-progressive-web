The Progressive Schism

"Progressivism" classically referred to an orientation toward open-ended progress: the belief that knowledge, reason, and criticism drive improvement. Today, many progressives equate progress only with social or moral outcomes rather than with the process that creates them.

When moral certainty replaces open inquiry, progressivism becomes regressive by preserving ideology and dogma instead of expanding knowledge.

Consider these concrete examples where specific outcomes are treated as unquestionable progressive goods, while the process of criticism and knowledge-generation is sidelined:

Speech Restrictions: Many modern progressives openly defend restrictions on speech in the name of preventing "harm." University bias-response teams investigate protected speech that merely causes offense. Instead of treating ideas as hypotheses to be tested, they are treated as threats to be contained.

COVID School Closures: Many progressive jurisdictions kept schools closed long after evidence supported reopening. Criticism of lockdown policies was framed as a moral failure rather than a legitimate scientific disagreement. What should have been a contested empirical question became a test of ideological loyalty.

Urban Homelessness Policies: In cities like San Francisco and Portland, homelessness and addiction policies became moral imperatives insulated from criticism even as overdose deaths rose. Requests for data or policy revision were often dismissed as lacking compassion, replacing learning with moral signaling.

Environmental Regulations: Many environmental progressives now use regulations designed for fossil-fuel infrastructure to block clean-energy projects, particularly nuclear power and transmission lines. The identity of "environmental protection" overrides the experimentation needed for genuine progress toward net zero.

This, we believe, is the core pathology within the modern American progressive movement. In order to sustain true progress, we must defend the means of progress: free thought, criticism, and decentralized discovery.

Defining Liberty Progressivism

Liberty Progressivism descends from the Enlightenment tradition and critical rationalism, pioneered by philosopher Karl Popper and powerfully elaborated by physicist David Deutsch. Critical rationalism holds that progress is the growth of knowledge through conjecture, criticism, and error correction.

At its core, Liberty Progressivism holds that the means of progress are more fundamental than any particular progressive outcome.

If we protect the mechanisms of error correction, good outcomes can emerge from the iterative process of learning. If we suppress those mechanisms, even the most well-intentioned goals become brittle dogmas that decay over time.

Following Deutsch, a Liberty Progressive treats freedom not as an abstract moral entitlement, but as a functional requirement for problem-solving. Freedom is what allows errors to be revealed, alternative solutions to be tested, and new explanations to be generated. Liberty is inseparable from progress.

The Three Means of Error Correction

To sustain progress, society must preserve these essential mechanisms:

1. Free Speech and Free Thought

The freedom to conjecture, criticize, debate, and dissent is the bedrock of any knowledge-creating civilization. Without free speech, society loses its ability to detect errors and learning collapses into obedience. Free inquiry is not merely a constitutional right; it is the operating principle of progress itself.

2. Distributed Power and the Right to Resist Coercion

Knowledge creation thrives when no single institution, ideology, or authority can dominate the terms of inquiry. Distributed power, including checks and balances, local autonomy, competitive markets, and the right to resist, prevents coercive monopolies on truth.

3. Decentralized Experimentation

Transformative progress rarely emerges from top-down prescriptions. It arises when individuals and groups generate solutions that no central authority could have anticipated. Markets, federalism, and local autonomy enable parallel problem-solving at scale.

In summary, a Liberty Progressive sees human flourishing as an open-ended project, not a destination or static moral equilibrium, but a process with infinite potential. What matters most is protecting the conditions for progress to continue indefinitely.

The Two Principal Axes

We establish the framework along two key principal components that explain the majority of variance in contemporary progressive political philosophy. This two-axis framework helps understand how Liberty Progressivism distinguishes from other forms of progressivism.

Where do you locate problem-solving capacity?

Liberty

Trust distributed problem-solving where progress emerges from free individuals and institutions experimenting, criticizing, and learning from failure. Power should be contestable, plural, and exit-based.

Statist

Trust centralized coordination where progress is directed through organized expertise, planning, and coordinated policy. May see individual liberties as impediments to achieving collective goals.

Axis 2: Reformer ‚Üî Revolutionary

How should change occur?

Reformer

Society is improvable through iterative change: test, learn, adapt. Embrace fallibilism, reversibility, and tolerance of dissent. Existing institutions can be vehicles for progress.

Revolutionary

Society is fundamentally broken and requires deconstruction and redesign. Moral certainty justifies urgent action. Progress is achieved once justice is implemented.

The extremes illustrate the distinctions, but individuals hold complex belief systems. Most people are centered somewhere between the extreme poles, allowing for nuance.

Why Liberty, Not Libertarian?

Liberty Progressive emphasizes freedom as the mechanism of progress, not as an ideology of extreme individualism. Classical libertarianism fits this view, but the term "libertarian" has drifted, often reduced to a caricature of anti-government dogma or ideological anarchism.

By using "liberty," we appeal to the Enlightenment tradition where freedom, reason, and knowledge creation were inseparable. Liberty, in this framework, is not incompatible with collective good; it's what makes the collective capable of problem-solving and knowledge creation.

Liberty Progressivism treats liberty as a tool for knowledge growth and progress, whereas libertarianism treats liberty as the final moral principle.

Living the Framework

Liberty Progressivism is not merely a diagnostic tool but a practical orientation we can live out and share. Here's how to put the framework into action:

Personal Level

  • Cultivate epistemic humility: Regularly seek out thoughtful disagreement. Treat policy debates as empirical questions and error-correction opportunities, not moral purity tests.
  • Champion free expression: Defend others' right to speak, even when you disagree, and model good-faith criticism.
  • Support decentralized discovery: Engage with the startup ecosystem, contribute to open-source projects, and champion local experiments rather than waiting for top-down solutions.

Communal and Cultural Level

  • Push institutions toward openness: Advocate for universities, media platforms, and scientific bodies to prioritize real viewpoint diversity and error-correction over ideological conformity.
  • Amplify Liberty Reformer voices: Highlight and support progressive leaders who combine ambitious goals with pragmatic, evidence-based, liberty-respecting methods.

Political Level

  • Advocate for structural safeguards: Support reforms that distribute power and protect error-correction mechanisms: ranked-choice voting, federalism, independent redistricting, strong free-speech protections, and antitrust enforcement.
  • Endorse compatible candidates: Prioritize those who reject censorship, moral policing, and centralized planning while pursuing ambitious aims.
  • Engage in persuasion, not cancellation: Focus on evidence, experimentation, and voluntary cooperation rather than coercive mandates or shame.
